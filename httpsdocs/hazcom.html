Purpose & Scope

This program establishes the minimum requirements for Clark Construction Group, LLC and Its Affiliates (“Clark”) for an effective hazard communication program.

  • Applies in its entirety to all Clark operations
  • Variances granted only by the Vice President of Safety
  • Also known as the “Right to Know” law
  • Requires a written hazard communication program at each workplace
  • Covers all employees, subcontractors, and multi-employer sites

Key Definitions

Click any term to see its full definition from the Clark HazCom Program.

Hazardous Chemical
Any classified physical or health hazard
Any chemical classified as a physical hazard or a health hazard, a simple asphyxiant, combustible dust, pyrophoric gas, or hazard not otherwise classified.
Container
Anything that holds a hazardous chemical
Any bag, barrel, bottle, box, can, cylinder, drum, reaction vessel, storage tank, or the like that contains a hazardous chemical.
Safety Data Sheet (SDS)
Written material about a hazardous chemical
Written or printed material concerning a hazardous chemical. Must follow the standardized 16-section format and be available at each workplace.
Signal Word
“Danger” or “Warning”
A word indicating relative severity of hazard. “Danger” is used for the more severe hazards, while “Warning” is used for the less severe.
Pictogram
Symbol conveying hazard information
A composition that may include a symbol plus other graphic elements, such as a border, background pattern, or color, intended to convey specific information about the hazards of a chemical.
Product Identifier
Unique name or number for a chemical
The name or number used for a hazardous chemical on a label or in the SDS. It permits cross-references among the chemical list, the label, and the SDS.
Hazard Statement
Describes the nature of the hazard
A statement assigned to a hazard class and category that describes the nature of the hazard(s) of a chemical, including, where appropriate, the degree of hazard.
Precautionary Statement
Recommended protective measures
A phrase that describes recommended measures to minimize or prevent adverse effects resulting from exposure to a hazardous chemical, or improper storage or handling.

Who Is Responsible?

Per the Clark HazCom Program — click each role to see their duties.

🛠
Project Superintendent
Overall responsibility for HazCom compliance
▼
  • Has overall responsibility for establishing and ensuring compliance with the HazCom procedure
  • Ensures a written HazCom program is developed, implemented, and maintained at the workplace
  • Maintains the list of hazardous chemicals known to be present, using product identifiers that reference the appropriate SDS
  • Ensures methods are in place to inform employees of non-routine task hazards and chemicals in unlabeled pipes
💻
Project Safety Manager
Implementing & monitoring the program
▼
  • Responsible for implementing and/or monitoring activities associated with the HazCom procedure
  • Maintains the SDS binders — copies of SDS may be obtained upon request
  • Coordinates employee information and training sessions
  • Monitors labeling compliance across the project
👷
All Managers & Supervisors
Enforce the procedure and ensure compliance
▼
  • Enforce this procedure and ensure that each employee follows it
  • Verify every container of hazardous chemicals in the workplace is labeled
  • Ensure SDS are accessible for each hazardous chemical on site
  • Conduct training when new chemical hazards are introduced to the work area
⚙️
All Employees
Your safety is your right and your responsibility
▼
  • Follow the HazCom procedure at all times
  • Read labels and SDS before using any chemical
  • Wear required PPE — no shortcuts
  • Never use a chemical from an unlabeled container
  • Report missing labels, SDS gaps, or spills to your supervisor
  • Attend all HazCom training sessions and ask questions

The Three Pillars of HazCom

Clark’s written program must describe how each of these will be met (Section 5):

🔖

Labeling

Every container of hazardous chemicals must be labeled with product identifier, signal word, hazard statements, pictograms, precautionary statements, and supplier info.

📄

Safety Data Sheets

SDS must be available for each hazardous chemical in the workplace. Maintained by the Project Safety Manager. Copies available upon request.

🎓

Employee Training

Effective information and training at initial assignment and whenever a new chemical hazard is introduced into the work area.

Multi-Employer Workplaces

Reading a GHS Label

Per Section 6(a), every container must have these 6 elements. Click each one.

Required Label Elements

① Product Identifier
Chemical name or product name — must permit cross-reference to the chemical list, label, and SDS.
② Signal Word
“DANGER” for more severe hazards or “WARNING” for less severe. Never both.
③ Hazard Statement(s)
Standardized phrases describing the nature and degree of each hazard.
④ Pictogram(s)
Red diamond-bordered symbols — one of the 8 standard hazard symbols per Figure C.1.
⑤ Precautionary Statement(s)
Prevention, response, storage, and disposal measures to minimize adverse effects.
⑥ Supplier Information
Name, address, and telephone number of the chemical manufacturer or importer.

Scenario: SDS Lookup

🚧 Job Site Scenario
A coworker splashes a chemical in their eyes while working on site. They’re in pain. You grab the SDS binder. Which section do you turn to first?
A Section 1 — Identification
B Section 4 — First-Aid Measures
C Section 8 — Exposure Controls / PPE
D Section 11 — Toxicological Information
Correct! Section 4 — First-Aid Measures gives you immediate treatment instructions by route of exposure (inhalation, skin, eye contact, ingestion), plus most important symptoms and indication of immediate medical attention needed.
In an emergency, go straight to Section 4 — First-Aid Measures. It gives you treatment instructions subdivided by route of exposure (inhalation, skin, eye contact, ingestion), plus symptoms and whether immediate medical attention is needed.
08

Employee Training

Per Section 6(c) of the Clark HazCom Program, employee training shall include:

  • Methods to detect the presence or release of a hazardous chemical (monitoring devices, visual appearance, odor)
  • Physical & health hazards of chemicals in the work area, including asphyxiation, combustible dust, and pyrophoric gas hazards
  • Protective measures employees can take — work practices, emergency procedures, and required PPE
  • Details of the HazCom program — how to read labels, the workplace labeling system, SDS order of information, and how to access SDS
⏰

When to Train

At time of initial assignment and whenever a new chemical hazard the employees have not previously been trained about is introduced into their work area.

📚

Information Required

Employees must be informed of: the requirements of HazCom, operations where hazardous chemicals are present, and location of the written program, chemical lists, and SDS.

📋

SDS Access

SDS binders are maintained by the Project Safety Manager. Copies may be obtained upon request at any time.

09

Non-Routine Tasks & Multi-Employer Sites

Per Section 5(a), the written HazCom program must address:

  • Methods to inform employees of hazards of non-routine tasks
  • Hazards associated with chemicals in unlabeled pipes in work areas

Multi-employer workplaces (Section 5(a)(2)) — when other employers’ workers may be exposed, Clark must also provide:

  • SDS access for each chemical the other employers’ employees may be exposed to
  • Precautionary measures needed during normal operations and foreseeable emergencies
  • Information about the labeling system used in the workplace
📣

Communication Is Key

On multi-employer sites, every contractor must know about chemicals that could affect their workers — even chemicals they don’t directly use.

📋

Unlabeled Pipes

The program must address how employees are informed about chemicals in unlabeled pipes in their work areas. Always check before cutting or opening any pipe.
